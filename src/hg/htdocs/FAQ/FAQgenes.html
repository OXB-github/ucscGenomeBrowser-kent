The basics

What is the difference between genes and transcripts?

There are a few general things that are true of any gene track. Every gene has a set of transcripts. Tracks are often called "Genes", e.g. "Ensembl Genes" or "UCSC Genes", but they really are transcripts on an assembly. Every transcript has an accession number, a sequence and, in the genome browser, also a list of exon chrom/start/end coordinates on a genome assembly. These transcript accessions are assigned to genes, so every transcript is assigned to one gene. Most of the genes have a symbol associated to them by the Human Gene Nomenclature Commitee (HGNC, formerly HUGO).

What are common gene tracks?

Originally, researchers sequenced cDNA sequences, submitted them to Genbank and the Genome Browser shows these in the Genbank track, or in the EST track, if the cDNA is just a single read from the 5' or 3' end. From the alignment of the cDNAs and ESTs, the NCBI RefSeq group would manually create a smaller set of representative transcripts. Automated programs like UCSC's or Ensembl's gene build software did the same, just in software, which is more systematic but also more error-prone. With the arrival of Gencode, Ensembl added a manual curation of their transcripts. Ensembl/Gencode accession numbers in the human genome start with ENST followed by a number. The genes have identifiers that start with ENSG and every ENSG has at least one ENST assigned to it. Manually curated Refseq transcript identifiers start with NM_ (coding) or NR_ (noncoding), followed by a number, or XM_ if they are predicted.

The differences

What is the difference between Ensembl and Gencode?

On the human and mouse genome, the identifiers, transcript sequences and exon coordinates of Ensembl and Gencode versions are identical. You can download the gene models either from the website gencodegenes.org or from http://ensembl.org. Additional information that are not directly transcript related, e.g. the linkouts to other databases, sometimes differ in the files that are distributed by Gencode and the gene model files you can find on the Ensembl website.
